The Life Cycle of the Cell

Cell division is essential for an organism to grow. The cell divides and DNA is replicated so that two identical cells with the exact same genetic information now exist.

Diagram showing the life cycle of the cell

The human cell life has two main phases; interphase which is the phase from cell formation to cell division and the mitotic phase when the cell reproduces by dividing into two identical cells.

Interphase is a time of cell growth and productive activity. After the new cell is “born”, the new cell begins to make proteins, carbohydrates and lipids which expand the size of the cell. The centrioles near the nucleus begin to replicate and prepare for cell division. The DNA inside the nucleus replicates during the second stage of interphase. The middle stage of interphase prepares the cell to commit itself to reproduction. During the last stage of interphase the cell continues to grow and make proteins.

Mitosis When a cell divides, the nucleus splits first followed by the cytoplasm (cytokinesis). Mitosis is divided into four sub-sections; prophase, metaphase, anaphase and telophase.

  • During prophase the DNA condenses into recognizable chromosomes, the nucleus disbands and the nuclear contents enter the cytoplasm.
  • During metaphase the chromosomes attach to the mitotic apparatus, a series of specially synthesized protein filaments anchored to opposite sides of the cell.
  • During anaphase, the chromosomes are pulled away from each other by the mitotic apparatus of the cell. Half of the chromosomes go to each side of the cell.
  • During telophase the nuclear membranes re-form, the cell’s contents are redistributed and the membrane “pinches off” to form two cells.
